# Part A — Ukraine's Monetary Regime: Chronology 2000–2025

## 1. Summary Table

|---|---|---|---|
| **2000–Aug 2008** | ~5.05 (fixed) | Conventional peg | NBU maintains a rigid band around 5.05 UAH/USD since 1998 post-crisis stabilisation. Minimal exchange-rate variation. Capital account partially open but FX interventions systematic. |
| **Sep 2008–Feb 2009** | 5.05 → ~8.0 (+58%) | Other managed / crawl-like | Global Financial Crisis: capital outflows, banking sector stress (bank runs, frozen deposits). | **Mar 2009–Jan 2014** | ~7.97–8.0 (re-pegged) | Stabilised arrangement | Return to de facto dollar peg at ~8 UAH/USD. NBU intervenes systematically to maintain the band. Monetary policy subordinated to exchange-rate anchor. Capital controls partially relaxed after 2010. |
| **Feb 2014–Feb 2015** | ~8 → ~26 (+225%) | Floating (de jure) / Other managed | Euromaidan revolution (Feb 2014), Crimea annexation (Mar 2014), Donbas war (Apr 2014). Massive capital flight, loss of reserves. NBU abandons peg (Feb 2014). Hyperinflationary pressure. Extensive capital controls: mandatory surrender of 75% of FX export earnings, limits on FX purchases, ban on early deposit withdrawal. IMF Extended Fund Facility ($17.5bn, Mar 2015). |
| **Mar 2015–Dec 2015** | ~20–26 (volatile) | Floating / Other managed | Transition and partial stabilisation. NBU drafts inflation-targeting framework. Capital controls remain strict. UAH stabilises around 22–24 after peak depreciation. |
| **Jan 2016–Jan 2022** | ~24–28 (managed float) | Floating (IT framework) | Official inflation targeting adopted (Aug 2015 announcement, 2016 implementation). First target: 12% ±3% end-2016, progressively lowered to 5% ±1% by 2019. NBU gains institutional independence (law reform 2015). Managed float: FX interventions used to smooth volatility, not fix the rate. Capital controls gradually eased 2016–2019, then re-tightened in 2020 (COVID). |
| **Feb 2022 – Jun 2022** | 29.25 (fixed) | Conventional peg | Full-scale Russian invasion (24 Feb 2022). NBU fixes rate at 29.25 UAH/USD on day of invasion. Wartime capital controls: ban on FX purchases for individuals, restrictions on cross-border transfers, mandatory surrender of 100% of FX revenues. |
| **Jul 2022 – mid-2023** | 36.57 (fixed) | Conventional peg | NBU devalues the fixed rate by ~25% (Jul 2022) to reduce distortions and align with parallel market. Rate held fixed at 36.57. Capital controls maintained. IMF Extended Arrangement ($15.6bn, Mar 2023). |
| **Mid-2023 – 2025** | 36.6–42+ (managed float) | Managed float / Stabilised arrangement | NBU introduces "managed flexibility" (Oct 2023): band approach, gradual depreciation allowed. FX controls progressively relaxed for current-account transactions. Rate drifts toward 40–42 UAH/USD by end-2024/2025. |

## 2. Argument: When Did Ukraine Have Genuine Monetary Sovereignty?

Ukraine's record of genuine monetary sovereignty is narrow and episodic. For the bulk of 2000–2014, the NBU operated a de facto dollar peg — the "impossible trinity" was binding: with open (or partially open) capital flows and a fixed exchange rate, the interest rate had no independent anchor. Monetary policy was reduced to reserve management in service of the peg. The three major devaluation episodes (2008–09, 2014–15, 2022) represent involuntary exits from the peg rather than exercises of monetary autonomy: in each case the NBU was forced off the peg by external shocks and reserve depletion. The only period in which Ukraine exercised meaningful, intentional monetary sovereignty is **2016–2021**, when the NBU operated a genuine inflation-targeting framework under a managed float, set interest rates in response to domestic conditions, and built institutional credibility — a period that coincides with the most sustained disinflation in Ukraine's post-Soviet history (from ~40% in 2015 to ~5% in 2019). Even this window was interrupted by COVID (2020) and closed definitively by the wartime fixed rate (2022). The implication for the counterfactual "treatment" of Euro Area membership is direct: the treatment effect is **not constant over time**. During peg periods (2000–2014, 2022–2023), Ukraine was already running a de facto external monetary anchor — joining the Euro Area would have replaced one anchor (the dollar) with another (the ECB), representing a *smaller* regime change. The counterfactual bite is concentrated in **two types of episodes**: (i) the devaluation crises (2008–09, 2014–15, 2022), where Euro Area membership would have removed the exchange-rate adjustment channel entirely, forcing internal devaluation instead; and (ii) the **2016–2021 inflation-targeting period**, where the ECB's superior credibility and lower inflation environment would have replaced the NBU's still-fragile domestic anchor with a more credible external one — likely delivering lower inflation than actually observed. Any counterfactual that treats the treatment as uniform over time implicitly assumes Ukraine had full monetary sovereignty throughout, which the chronology above demonstrates to be incorrect.
