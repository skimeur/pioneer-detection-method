## 2. Argument: When Did Ukraine Have Genuine Monetary Sovereignty?

Ukraine's record of genuine monetary sovereignty is narrow. For most of 2000–2014, the NBU operated a de facto dollar peg — with fixed exchange rate and partially open capital account, the impossible trinity left no room for an independent interest rate. Monetary policy was essentially reserve management in service of the peg. The three major devaluation episodes (2008–09, 2014–15, 2022) were involuntary exits driven by reserve depletion and external shocks, not deliberate policy decisions. The only period of meaningful, intentional monetary sovereignty is **2016–2021**: the NBU ran a genuine IT framework under a managed float, set rates in response to domestic conditions, and achieved the most sustained disinflation in Ukraine's post-Soviet history (from ~40% in 2015 to ~5% in 2019). Even that window was partly interrupted by COVID in 2020, and the wartime fixed rate closed it in 2022. This matters for the counterfactual: the "treatment" of Euro Area membership is **not constant over time**. During peg periods, Ukraine was already anchored externally — joining the EA would have swapped one anchor for another, which is a smaller change. The counterfactual really bites in two places: (i) the devaluation crises, where the exchange-rate channel would have been unavailable and real adjustment would have had to go through wages and output; and (ii) the 2016–2021 IT period, where the ECB's superior credibility would plausibly have delivered lower inflation than the still-fragile NBU framework. A counterfactual that assumes uniform treatment throughout the sample would be misspecified given this chronology.
